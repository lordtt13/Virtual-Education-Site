DESIGN OF HALF AND FULL WAVE RECTIFIER

  
      

AIM:

To Design a Half and Full wave rectifier and observe the input and output waveforms voltage and current

SOFTWARE REQUIRED:

OrCAD-PSpice/MATLAB-SIMULINK

THEORY:

Half Wave Bridge Rectifier:

A simple Half Wave Rectifier is nothing more than a single PN junction diode connected in series to the load resistor. When a single rectifier diode unit is placed in series with the load across an ac supply, it converts a positive half cycle alternating voltage into uni-directional pulsating voltage. This is called half-wave rectification.

During the positive half cycle of the input sinusoidal voltage, the PN junction diode is forward biased. As a result, a current 'I' flows through the load resistance, RL. The forward biased diode offers a very low resistance and hence the voltage drop across it is very small. Thus, the voltage appearing across the load is practically the same as the input voltage for positive half cycle.

During the negative half cycle of the input sinusoidal voltage, the PN junction diode is reversed biased . As a result, Practically no current flows through the circuit and almost no voltage is developed across the resistor. All input voltage appears across the diode itself.

Hence, we conclude that when the input voltage is going through its positive half cycle, output voltage is almost the same as the input voltage and during the negative half cycle no voltage is available across the load.

Full Wave Bridge Rectifier:

A full Wave Bridge Rectifier is having four PN junction diodes connected in bridge form as shown in Figure. When a bridge rectifier unit is placed between the AC source and load, it converts the both half cycles of alternating voltage into unidirectional pulsating voltage. This is called full-wave rectification.

During the positive half cycle of the input sinusoidal voltage, the diodes D1 and D2 are forward biased and D3 and D4 are reverse biased. As a result, a current I flows through the load resistance, RL. The current flow direction is from D1--RL--D2. The voltage appearing across the load is practically the same as the input voltage for positive half cycle.

During the negative half cycle of the input sinusoidal voltage, the diodes D3 and D4 are forward biased and D1 and D2 are reverse biased. As a result, a current I flows through the load resistance, RL. The current flow direction is from D3--RL--D4. The voltage appearing across the load is practically the same as the input voltage for negative half cycle.

Hence, we conclude that both positive and negative half cycles, the bridge rectifiers converts into unidirection pulsating voltage.
