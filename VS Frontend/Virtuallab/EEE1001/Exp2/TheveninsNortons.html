VERIFICATION OF THEVENIN'S AND NORTON'S THEOREM

  
      

AIM:

Solve the given circuit by Thevenin's and Norton's Theorem and verify with OrCAD-PSpice/MATLAB software.

SOFTWARE REQUIRED:

OrCAD-PSpice/MATLAB-SIMULINK

THEORY:

Thevenin's Theorem:

This Theorem is applied to calculate the current flow through particular branch resistance and the voltage across the two nodes by converting the given single port linear network to an equivalent Voltage source in series with an equivalent Resistance. Initially it is adopted only for DC circuits and later the theorem is also applicable for AC networks by replacing the resistance to impedance.

Other Web Links: Link-1, Link-2

Norton's Theorem:

Norton's Theorem can also be applied to calculate the the circuit parameters like current and voltage of the load resistance by converting the given single port linear network to an equivalent Current source in parallel with an equivalent Resistance.

Other Web Links: Link-1, Link-2

PROCEDURE:

Thevenin's Theorem:

1. In the given circuit, remove the load resistance from the circuit and name the terminals 'a' & 'b'.

2. Across the terminals a,b, Calculate equivalent resistance RTh by short circuiting the volatge sources and open circuiting the current sources.

3. By keeping all the sources, Calculate the open circuit voltage VTh across the terminals a, b.

4. Draw the Thevenin's equivalent circuit and connect the removed load resistance at the terminals of a, b for calculating the current flows or voltage across it.

Norton's Theorem:

1. In the given circuit, remove the load resistance from the circuit and name the terminals 'a' & 'b'.

2. Across the terminals a,b, Calculate equivalent resistance RN by short circuiting the volatge sources and open circuiting the current sources.

3. By keeping all the sources, Calculate the short circuit Current IN by short circuiting the terminals a, b.

4. Draw the Norton's equivalent circuit and connect the removed load resistance at the terminals of a, b for calculating the current flows or voltage across it.
